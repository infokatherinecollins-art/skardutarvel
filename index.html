Northern Pakistan

A valley built between giants

Skardu sits at the confluence of the Indus and Shigar rivers, ringed by some of the highest mountains on Earth. It is the last proper town before the Karakoram opens into glacier and rock, which is why almost every serious expedition into the range, K2 included, begins its final approach here.

The town itself is easy to underestimate. Its real character shows in the valley around it: apricot orchards climbing the hillsides, Balti forts built by a line of local rulers, and rivers running a pale, glacial green. Skardu is also the practical hinge for the whole region, the place travelers restock, arrange permits, and pick up a jeep before heading further into Shigar, Khaplu, or the Deosai plateau.

What draws people back is the range of it. In a single week you can stand on a cold desert dune, float across a lake ringed by pine forest, and sleep at the edge of a plateau grazed by brown bears. Few places in Pakistan compress that much landscape into so short a drive.

Where to go

Six places worth the drive

Skardu's attractions are spread across a wide valley, so most visitors base themselves in town and take day trips out to lakes, forts, and the plateau.

K2 base camp trek

The classic route into the Karakoram, following the Baltoro glacier past Concordia, where four of the world's fourteen highest peaks meet in one amphitheater of ice.

Deosai National Park

The second-highest plateau in the world, open only in summer. Wide grassland, brown bears, and wildflowers stretching to the horizon.

Shangrila and Lower Kachura Lake

A pine-fringed lake often called Lower Kachura, with water so still it mirrors the surrounding cliffs. A short, easy trip from town.

Shigar and Khaplu forts

Restored royal residences of the old Balti kingdoms, built from timber and stone, now working heritage guesthouses set among orchards.

Satpara Lake

A glacial reservoir just outside town, backed by bare granite peaks, popular for boating and as the launch point toward Deosai.

Skardu cold desert

Sand dunes on the banks of the Indus, framed by snow peaks, an odd and photogenic contrast unique to this stretch of the valley.

Planning

The valley changes with the season

Skardu is a year-round destination, but what you can reach depends heavily on when you go.

March – April

Spring

Apricot and cherry blossoms cover the lower valley and orchard villages, with mild days and cool nights before the trekking season begins.

June – August

Summer

Peak season. Deosai opens once the snow clears, K2 base camp treks run, and roads to Shigar and Khaplu are at their most reliable.

September – October

Autumn

Poplar and willow trees turn gold against the grey rock, days stay clear, and the summer crowds thin out considerably.

November – February

Winter

Snow closes the high passes and Deosai, but Skardu town stays accessible by air, offering a quiet, low-cost look at the valley.

Logistics

Getting to Skardu

By air

Daily flights connect Islamabad to Skardu Airport in about 55 minutes, weather permitting. Morning flights are more reliable, since clouds tend to build over the mountains by early afternoon. This is the fastest and most common way in.

By road

The overland route follows the Karakoram Highway through Chilas and along the Indus, roughly 14 to 16 hours from Islamabad. It's a longer trip, but it passes Nanga Parbat viewpoints and pairs naturally with a stop in Hunza on the way north.

Getting around locally

Hired jeeps and local guides are the standard way to reach Deosai, Shigar, and Khaplu, since public transport is limited and several routes need 4x4 vehicles, particularly after rain or snowmelt.

Where to stay

Skardu town has guesthouses and hotels across a range of budgets, while Shigar and Khaplu forts offer heritage stays for travelers who want to base further out in the valley.
